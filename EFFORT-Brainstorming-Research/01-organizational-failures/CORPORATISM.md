# Corporatism Through the Hedge-and-Crack Lens

## Deep Research: How Corporate Structure Was Designed, How It Breaks, and What Comes Next

> Organizations exist to coordinate humans in society with minimal friction. They have built-in hedges against human nature. The system fails when human nature breaks through the cracks in those hedges, causing friction that compounds until the system breaks.

This document applies the hedge-and-crack framework to the corporate form -- the dominant organizational system of the modern world. The goal is not anti-corporate polemic but honest analysis: what does the corporate structure do well, where does it fail, and why?

---

## 1. The Hedges -- What Corporate Structure Was Designed to Protect Against

### 1.1 Coase and the Transaction Cost Hedge

**The Problem:** Coordinating economic activity through pure market transactions is expensive. Every exchange requires finding counterparties, negotiating terms, writing contracts, monitoring performance, and enforcing compliance. Ronald Coase, in "The Nature of the Firm" (1937), asked the question nobody had formally asked: if markets are so efficient, why do firms exist at all?

**The Hedge:** Firms exist because they internalize transaction costs. Instead of negotiating a new contract every time you need a task done, you hire an employee. Instead of searching the market for every input, you build a supply chain. The firm replaces thousands of market transactions with an authority relationship -- the manager directs, the employee executes. This is cheaper when:

- **Search and information costs are high** -- finding the right person for a specific task in the open market takes time and money.
- **Bargaining and decision costs are high** -- negotiating terms for every transaction is wasteful when you need ongoing coordination.
- **Monitoring and enforcement costs are high** -- it is cheaper to supervise an employee than to write and enforce a complete contract for every piece of work.

**The human nature problem being hedged:** Without firms, coordination would be subject to constant opportunism, information asymmetry, and hold-up problems. Every transaction becomes a negotiation where both parties are incentivized to extract maximum value and share minimum information. The firm creates a zone where people cooperate under authority rather than haggling under market pressure.

**What Coase got right:** The theory explains firm size elegantly. A firm grows until the cost of organizing one more transaction internally equals the cost of doing it on the market. It also explains why firms shrink when communication technology improves -- the telephone, the internet, and now AI each reduced transaction costs and enabled smaller, more distributed coordination.

**Key insight for the framework:** The firm is fundamentally a hedge against the friction of market-based human coordination. It trades one set of human-nature problems (opportunism in markets) for another set (hierarchy, bureaucracy, agency problems). The question is whether the trade is worth it -- and whether it stays worth it as technology changes the calculus.

### 1.2 Limited Liability -- Hedging Against Risk Aversion

**The Problem:** Humans are loss-averse. Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory (1979) established that losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains in human decision-making. Without protection from catastrophic personal loss, rational people would never invest in risky ventures. Economic growth would stall.

**The Hedge:** The limited liability corporation, formalized in Britain's Joint Stock Companies Act of 1855 and its successors, creates a legal fiction: the corporation is a "person" separate from its owners. If the business fails, investors lose their investment but nothing more. Their homes, savings, and personal assets are protected.

**The human nature problem being hedged:** Pure risk aversion. Without limited liability, the only people who would start or invest in businesses are those who either have nothing to lose or are pathologically overconfident. Limited liability democratized investment by capping downside while leaving upside unlimited. This is the structural foundation of modern capitalism -- it enabled the pooling of capital from thousands of small investors who would never have risked their personal wealth on a single venture.

**Historical evidence:** Before limited liability, business failure often meant debtor's prison. The South Sea Bubble (1720) ruined thousands precisely because investors bore unlimited personal liability. The legal innovation of limited liability unleashed the Industrial Revolution's capital formation -- the railway boom of the 1840s in Britain was funded by limited liability investment that would have been unthinkable under older partnership law.

**The crack it opens:** By separating ownership from personal consequence, limited liability also separates ownership from accountability. When failure costs you everything, you govern carefully. When failure costs you only your investment, you may be more willing to tolerate -- or even encourage -- reckless behavior by management, especially if the upside is large. This moral hazard is the seed from which the principal-agent problem grows.

### 1.3 Boards of Directors -- Hedging Against Management Self-Dealing

**The Problem:** Once ownership is dispersed across thousands of shareholders (enabled by limited liability), no single owner can effectively oversee management. Berle and Means documented this in "The Modern Corporation and Private Property" (1932) -- the separation of ownership and control creates an opening for managers to serve themselves rather than shareholders.

**The Hedge:** The board of directors, elected by shareholders, serves as a governance intermediary. The board hires, fires, and compensates the CEO. It approves major strategic decisions. It is supposed to represent shareholder interests and hold management accountable.

**The human nature problem being hedged:** Power-seeking and self-dealing. Without a board, the CEO answers to nobody. The board is the institutional check on executive authority -- the corporate equivalent of separation of powers in government.

**Fiduciary duty** reinforces this hedge legally. Directors and officers owe a fiduciary duty of care (make informed decisions) and loyalty (don't self-deal) to shareholders. This is not just a norm -- it is an enforceable legal obligation, with courts as backstop. Delaware corporate law (where more than 60% of Fortune 500 companies are incorporated) has developed over a century of case law precisely to adjudicate these duties.

### 1.4 Competition Between Firms -- The External Hedge

**The Problem:** Even with internal governance, a firm can become complacent, inefficient, or extractive. Internal checks may be captured or weakened.

**The Hedge:** Market competition. If a firm becomes bloated, overpriced, or low-quality, competitors will take its customers. Adam Smith's invisible hand -- firms must serve customers well or die. Competition is the ultimate external discipline, a hedge that operates regardless of internal governance quality.

**The human nature problem being hedged:** Complacency, rent-seeking, and organizational inertia. Competition forces continuous improvement even when internal incentives would favor coasting. Joseph Schumpeter called this "creative destruction" -- the relentless cycle of innovation displacing incumbents.

**Reinforcing mechanisms:** Antitrust law (Sherman Act 1890, Clayton Act 1914, FTC Act 1914) was designed to preserve competition when natural market forces proved insufficient. The recognition was explicit: left unchecked, the winners of competition would destroy competition itself.

### 1.5 Employment Contracts -- Hedging Against Instability

**The Problem:** In pure market coordination, workers face radical uncertainty. Every day could be their last day of income. This instability causes underinvestment in firm-specific skills (why learn your employer's systems if you might be gone tomorrow?), excessive job-searching costs, and the social instability of a fully casualized workforce.

**The Hedge:** Employment contracts provide stability and predictability for both sides. The worker commits time and effort; the firm commits to regular compensation and working conditions. Labor law (minimum wage, overtime, anti-discrimination, wrongful termination protections) further stabilizes the relationship.

**The human nature problem being hedged:** On the employer side -- exploitation. Without labor protections, the power asymmetry between employer and employee invites abuse. On the worker side -- free-riding and shirking. The employment contract creates mutual commitment that reduces opportunism on both sides.

### 1.6 Summary: The Corporate Hedging System

| Hedge | Human Nature Problem | Mechanism |
|-------|---------------------|-----------|
| **Firm organization** (Coase) | Opportunism, information asymmetry in market transactions | Authority relationship replaces market negotiation |
| **Limited liability** | Risk aversion preventing investment | Cap downside to enable capital pooling |
| **Board of directors** | Management self-dealing (power-seeking) | Elected representatives of owners oversee managers |
| **Fiduciary duty** | Self-dealing, disloyalty | Legal obligation with court enforcement |
| **Market competition** | Complacency, rent-seeking, inertia | External pressure from rivals and new entrants |
| **Antitrust law** | Monopolistic capture of markets | Government enforcement of competitive conditions |
| **Employment contracts** | Exploitation (employer), shirking (worker) | Mutual commitment with legal protections |

**What corporations do well:** This is an impressive system. It has enabled coordination at scales unimaginable before the modern era. The limited liability corporation funded the railways, electrification, telecommunications, computing, and the internet. It created the middle class through stable employment. It drove innovation through competitive pressure. Any honest analysis must acknowledge that the corporate form is one of humanity's most successful organizational inventions.

**The question is not whether corporations work.** They manifestly do, for a while, under the right conditions. The question is: where do they fail, why do they fail, and is the failure pattern systematic?

## 2. The Cracks -- Where Human Nature Breaks Through

### 2.1 C-Suite Extraction and the Principal-Agent Problem (Power-Seeking)

**The human tendency:** Power-seeking -- the drive to accumulate control, resources, and status beyond what is necessary for the role. This is one of the most robust findings in organizational psychology and one of the oldest observations in political philosophy (Acton's dictum: "Power tends to corrupt, and absolute power corrupts absolutely").

**The hedge it defeats:** Boards of directors, fiduciary duty, shareholder oversight.

**The mechanism of failure:**

Jensen and Meckling's seminal 1976 paper "Theory of the Firm: Managerial Behavior, Agency Costs and Ownership Structure" formalized the **principal-agent problem**: when the people making decisions (agents/managers) are not the people bearing the consequences (principals/shareholders), the agents will systematically favor their own interests. The agency costs include monitoring costs (what the principal spends watching the agent), bonding costs (what the agent spends to demonstrate alignment), and residual loss (the remaining gap between agent behavior and principal-optimal behavior).

**How boards became captured:**

The theoretical hedge -- boards oversee management -- broke down through several mechanisms:

1. **CEO influence over board selection.** In practice, the CEO often has enormous influence over who gets nominated to the board. The nominating committee is supposed to be independent, but social networks, reciprocity, and deference to the sitting CEO mean boards are frequently populated with the CEO's allies, friends, and fellow travelers. A 2013 study by Coles, Daniel, and Naveen found that CEOs with more influence over the board selection process received higher compensation.

2. **Interlocking directorates.** Board members sit on multiple boards simultaneously. This creates a web of reciprocal relationships: "I'll approve your pay package if you approve mine." Research by Hallock (1997) found a direct positive correlation between CEO pay and the presence of interlocking directorates. The old-boy network is not a metaphor -- it is a measurable structural feature of corporate governance.

3. **Information asymmetry.** The CEO controls the information flow to the board. Board members typically serve part-time, meet quarterly, and rely on management-prepared materials. They have neither the time nor the independent information infrastructure to truly oversee a complex organization. The CEO knows more about the business than the board does, which inverts the intended power relationship.

4. **Social dynamics and conformity pressure.** Boards are small groups of high-status individuals. Research on groupthink (Janis, 1972) shows that such groups tend toward consensus, avoid conflict, and defer to the leader. Board meetings are often performative -- the real decisions are made before the meeting, and dissent is socially costly.

**CEO compensation data:**

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) has tracked CEO compensation relative to typical worker pay using consistent methodology:

| 1995 | 123:1 | Stock options become dominant compensation tool |

The "granted" measure is somewhat lower but shows the same trajectory.*

**The stock option distortion:** The explosion in CEO pay began in earnest in the 1990s when stock options became the dominant form of executive compensation. The theoretical justification was alignment: if CEOs are paid in stock, their interests align with shareholders. In practice, stock options created perverse incentives:

- **Short-term stock price manipulation.** CEOs have incentive to do anything that inflates the stock price before their options vest -- share buybacks, accounting games, cost-cutting that sacrifices long-term health for short-term earnings.
- **Asymmetric risk.** Options are valuable when the stock goes up but cost nothing (to the executive) when it goes down. This creates a one-way bet that encourages excessive risk-taking. Heads I win, tails I try again with a new grant.
- **The repricing game.** When stock prices fell, many companies simply repriced their executives' options to lower strike prices, eliminating the accountability mechanism entirely.

**Share buybacks as extraction mechanism:** In 2023, S&P 500 companies spent approximately $795 billion on share buybacks. Before 1982, share buybacks were effectively illegal, treated as market manipulation under SEC rules. The SEC's Rule 10b-18 (1982) created a safe harbor for buybacks, and the practice exploded. The mechanical effect: buybacks reduce share count, which increases earnings per share, which inflates stock price, which triggers executive compensation windfalls. William Lazonick of the University of Massachusetts has documented how buybacks transfer wealth from the productive enterprise to executives and shareholders, arguing that corporations have shifted from "retain and reinvest" to "downsize and distribute."

**Concrete examples:**

- **Boeing (2010s-2020s):** Between 2013 and 2019, Boeing spent approximately $43 billion on share buybacks while cutting engineering staff and pressuring suppliers on costs. Two 737 MAX crashes killed 346 people. Internal communications revealed engineers raising safety concerns that were overridden by management focused on production speed and cost reduction. The board approved the buybacks; the CEO's compensation was tied to stock performance. This is the principal-agent problem made lethal.

- **Golden parachutes.** A study by Bebchuk, Cohen, and Wang found that firms with entrenched management and weak governance consistently paid higher executive compensation. Golden parachutes -- massive severance packages triggered by termination or acquisition -- insulate executives from accountability for failure. When Marissa Mayer left Yahoo in 2017 after a widely criticized tenure, her total compensation package was estimated at over $239 million for five years of work that ended with the company's sale and effective dissolution.

- **GE under Jack Welch and successors.** Welch was celebrated as the model CEO, driving GE's stock price from $14 billion market cap in 1981 to $410 billion in 2000. But much of this "value creation" was built on GE Capital's opaque financial engineering, aggressive accounting, and a culture that rewarded meeting quarterly numbers at any cost. When the financial engineering unwound, GE destroyed over $300 billion in market value and was removed from the Dow Jones Industrial Average in 2018. The extraction was real (Welch's retirement package was over $400 million); the value creation was partially illusory.

**Key research:**

- Jensen & Meckling (1976), "Theory of the Firm" -- foundational paper on agency costs
- Bebchuk & Fried (2004), "Pay Without Performance" -- documented how executive compensation bears little relation to firm performance
- Lazonick (2014), "Profits Without Prosperity" -- HBR article on how stock buybacks undermine the productive economy
- Piketty (2014), "Capital in the Twenty-First Century" -- broader context on capital vs. labor returns

### 2.2 Regulatory Capture and Moat-Building (Rent-Seeking)

**The human tendency:** Rent-seeking -- extracting value without creating it, typically by manipulating the rules of the game rather than competing within them. This is the political expression of economic self-interest: when it is cheaper to lobby a regulator than to build a better product, rational actors will lobby.

**The hedge it defeats:** Market competition, antitrust law.

**The mechanism of failure:**

The competitive hedge works only if the competitive playing field remains open. Regulatory capture -- the process by which regulated industries come to control their regulators -- systematically tilts the field. George Stigler formalized this in his "Theory of Economic Regulation" (1971), arguing that regulation is typically acquired by the industry and designed and operated primarily for the industry's benefit.

**The revolving door:**

The mechanism is human, not abstract. The same people rotate between regulatory agencies and the industries they regulate:

- A 2021 study by the Project on Government Oversight (POGO) found that over the preceding two decades, more than 1,600 former federal employees registered as lobbyists within two years of leaving government, and the actual number moving into industry roles (not all of which require lobbying registration) is far higher.
- At the SEC, it has been routine for enforcement attorneys to leave for lucrative positions at the law firms and financial institutions they were supposed to be regulating. A 2013 study by deHaan, Kedia, Koh, and Rajgopal found that SEC enforcement actions were systematically lower against firms that employed ex-SEC staff.
- The FCC has historically drawn commissioners from the telecom industry and seen them return to it. Former FCC Chairman Ajit Pai joined a private equity fund focused on telecom investments after leaving the commission.

**Lobbying spending data:**

Corporate lobbying in the United States has grown dramatically:

|------|--------------------------------------|
| 1998 | $1.45 billion |
| 2005 | $2.44 billion |
| 2010 | $3.52 billion |

This counts only registered federal lobbying. State-level lobbying, issue advertising, think-tank funding, and other influence channels are estimated to multiply the total by 3-5x. The pharmaceutical industry alone spent $374 million on federal lobbying in 2022, more than any other industry.

**The return on investment is extraordinary.** A 2009 study by Duchin and Sosyura found that firms with political connections were significantly more likely to receive TARP bailout funds during the 2008 financial crisis. Raghuram Rajan (former IMF chief economist) documented in "Fault Lines" (2010) how the financial industry's political influence contributed directly to the deregulation that enabled the 2008 crisis.

**Moat-building mechanisms:**

Beyond direct regulatory capture, firms build competitive moats through:

1. **Patent system abuse.** The patent system was designed to protect innovation (a legitimate hedge against free-riding on R&D investment). But it has been weaponized for rent-seeking. "Patent trolls" -- entities that produce nothing but acquire patents to extract licensing fees -- accounted for an estimated 62% of all patent lawsuits in the U.S. in a 2014 study by RPX Corporation. The pharmaceutical industry routinely uses "patent evergreening" -- making trivial modifications to existing drugs to extend patent protection. Humira (adalimumab), the world's best-selling drug, was protected by a thicket of over 100 patents that delayed generic competition for years, costing the U.S. healthcare system an estimated $19 billion in excess costs.

2. **Platform lock-in.** Tech platforms achieve natural monopoly through network effects, then use their position to suppress competition. The European Commission's antitrust cases against Google (three separate fines totaling over EUR 8 billion between 2017 and 2019) documented how Google used its search dominance to favor its own shopping service, how it used Android's market power to pre-install its apps, and how it used its advertising platform to exclude competitors. Microsoft's earlier antitrust battles (DOJ v. Microsoft, 2001) established the pattern: bundle, leverage, exclude.

3. **Regulatory complexity as barrier.** Large firms can absorb the cost of complex regulations that smaller competitors cannot. Dodd-Frank financial regulation, whatever its merits for systemic risk, increased compliance costs dramatically -- JPMorgan Chase reportedly spent $2 billion annually on compliance. Small community banks could not absorb equivalent costs, accelerating banking consolidation. The number of FDIC-insured commercial banks fell from approximately 8,300 in 2000 to approximately 4,100 by 2023. The large banks did not oppose regulation per se -- they opposed regulation that would actually constrain them, while welcoming regulation that raised barriers to entry.

**Case study -- Telecommunications:**

The telecom industry provides a textbook case of regulatory capture creating persistent moats. Despite massive public subsidies for broadband deployment (the U.S. has provided over $100 billion in subsidies and tax breaks for broadband infrastructure since the 1990s), broadband competition remains limited. Approximately 80% of U.S. households have only one or two options for high-speed broadband. The incumbent providers -- AT&T, Comcast, Verizon, Charter -- have successfully lobbied state legislatures to pass laws preventing municipalities from building their own broadband networks (over 20 states have such restrictions). This is rent-seeking in its purest form: using political power to prevent competition rather than winning through superior service.

**Key research:**

- Stigler (1971), "The Theory of Economic Regulation" -- foundational paper on regulatory capture
- Lessig (2011), "Republic, Lost" -- comprehensive account of institutional corruption in U.S. politics
- Rajan (2010), "Fault Lines" -- financial industry regulatory capture and the 2008 crisis
- Bessen (2015), "Learning by Doing" -- how firms use regulation to build competitive moats

### 2.3 The Peter Principle and Bureaucratic Bloat (Status-Seeking)

**The human tendency:** Status-seeking -- the drive to acquire rank, titles, and position as ends in themselves, independent of (and sometimes counter to) productive contribution. This combines with empire-building -- the incentive of every manager to expand their team, because team size correlates with status, compensation, and organizational power.

**The hedge it defeats:** Organizational efficiency, competitive pressure.

**The mechanism of failure:**

**The Peter Principle:** Laurence J. Peter and Raymond Hull articulated this in 1969: "In a hierarchy, every employee tends to rise to his level of incompetence." Promotion is based on performance in the current role, but the skills required at the next level are often different. A brilliant engineer becomes a mediocre manager. A brilliant manager becomes a terrible VP. The organization fills with people who have been promoted past their competence.

Is this just a joke? No. A 2018 study by Benson, Li, and Shue ("Promotions and the Peter Principle") used data from 214 firms and over 53,000 sales employees to test the Peter Principle empirically. They found that firms systematically promote top sales performers into management -- and these high-performing salespeople become significantly worse managers than average-performing salespeople who were promoted. The effect was large: a doubling in pre-promotion sales was associated with a 7.5% decline in the performance of the promoted manager's subordinates. The study confirmed the Peter Principle with hard data: organizations promote based on current performance, not managerial aptitude, and the result is systematic misallocation of talent.

**Empire-building and bureaucratic bloat:**

C. Northcote Parkinson observed in 1955 that "work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion" (Parkinson's Law). The organizational corollary: bureaucracy expands regardless of the amount of work to be done. Every manager has incentive to add headcount because:

1. More direct reports = higher pay grade in most corporate compensation structures.
2. Larger teams = more organizational power (budget authority, meeting invitations, decision-making influence).
3. Hiring is visible and rewarded; firing is painful and punished. The asymmetry ensures teams grow but rarely shrink voluntarily.

David Graeber's "Bullshit Jobs" (2018) provided ethnographic evidence that a significant portion of the modern workforce performs work that even the workers themselves consider pointless. Graeber categorized these as: flunkies (make someone else look important), goons (exist only because competitors have them), duct tapers (fix problems that shouldn't exist), box tickers (create the appearance of compliance without substance), and taskmasters (manage people who don't need managing). A YouGov poll he cited found that 37% of British workers believed their job made no meaningful contribution to the world.

**Administrative overhead data:**

The growth of administrative overhead is documented across multiple sectors:

**Healthcare:** The United States spends dramatically more on healthcare administration than other developed nations. A 2019 study by Himmelstein, Campbell, and Woolhandler in the Annals of Internal Medicine found that administrative costs consumed 34.2% of total U.S. healthcare expenditures ($812 billion in 2017), compared to 16.7% in Canada. Between 1975 and 2010, the number of healthcare administrators in the U.S. grew 3,200% while the number of physicians grew only 150%. The ratio of administrators to physicians went from roughly 1:1 to approximately 10:1.

**Higher education:** Between 1975 and 2015, the number of full-time administrators at U.S. colleges and universities grew by 85%, while the number of full-time faculty grew by only 51% (and much of the faculty growth was in non-tenure-track positions). At many universities, administrators now outnumber faculty. The share of university spending going to administration versus instruction has shifted markedly -- a 2014 Delta Cost Project study found that administrative spending per student grew 61% in real terms between 1998 and 2014, while instructional spending grew only 39%.

**Finance:** The financial sector's share of U.S. GDP grew from approximately 4% in 1970 to over 8% by 2006 -- a doubling that Thomas Philippon (NYU) documented was not associated with proportional improvements in financial intermediation efficiency. The cost of financial intermediation remained roughly constant from 1900 to 1970, then rose significantly despite massive advances in information technology that should have reduced it. The growth went largely to headcount, compensation, and organizational complexity.

**The Graeber hypothesis and evidence:**

Graeber argued that bullshit jobs proliferate because of the managerial feudalism inherent in modern corporations: status is measured by the number of people beneath you, so managers create positions to grow their fiefdoms, and those new positions create work for other new positions in an expanding bureaucratic universe. The work is not productive -- it is organizational metabolism, the organization feeding itself.

Evidence is difficult to quantify precisely because self-assessment of job meaningfulness is subjective. However, multiple surveys corroborate the pattern:

- A Gallup State of the Global Workplace survey (2022) found that only 23% of employees worldwide were "engaged" at work. 59% were "quiet quitting" (doing the minimum), and 18% were "actively disengaged." While engagement is not the same as job meaningfulness, the correlation is strong.
- A 2015 Harvard Business Review study found that, on average, senior executives spend only 27% of their time on tasks directly related to the company's strategic priorities. The rest was consumed by email, administrative tasks, and internal meetings with no clear decision outcome.

**Key research:**

- Peter & Hull (1969), "The Peter Principle"
- Benson, Li, & Shue (2018), "Promotions and the Peter Principle" -- empirical validation
- Graeber (2018), "Bullshit Jobs: A Theory"
- Parkinson (1955), "Parkinson's Law"
- Himmelstein et al. (2019), healthcare administrative costs study

### 2.4 Organizational Inertia and the Innovator's Dilemma (Loss Aversion)

**The human tendency:** Loss aversion -- the deep preference for protecting what exists over pursuing what might be better. At the organizational level, this becomes structural: every existing product, process, and department has human defenders whose jobs, status, and identity depend on the status quo continuing.

**The hedge it defeats:** Market competition (the firm becomes unable to respond to competitive threats).

**The mechanism of failure:**

Clayton Christensen's "The Innovator's Dilemma" (1997) documented a repeating pattern: successful companies are displaced not by competitors who do the same thing better, but by competitors who do a different thing that initially looks worse. The incumbent cannot respond because:

1. **Existing customers don't want it.** The disruptive innovation initially serves a different, less profitable market. The incumbent's best customers -- the ones who drive revenue -- explicitly tell management not to pursue it.
2. **Financial metrics reject it.** The disruptive product has lower margins, smaller addressable market, and worse near-term financial projections. Every rational analysis says "don't do this."
3. **Organizational structure opposes it.** The people who run the existing business control resources and have the most organizational power. They have every incentive to starve the disruptive effort and feed their own.
4. **Identity and culture resist it.** "We are a [film/phone/retail] company" becomes a prison. The organization's self-concept -- which is really the collective identity of its people -- cannot accommodate a fundamental redefinition.

**The case studies:**

**Kodak:** This is the canonical example. Kodak engineer Steve Sasson invented the digital camera in 1975. Kodak held key digital imaging patents. But Kodak's business was film -- high-margin, chemical-based, with a massive installed base. Digital photography would destroy the film business. Kodak's response was perfectly rational from an agency-theory perspective: the people running the film division (which was essentially the whole company) fought to protect their business, their teams, and their careers. Kodak filed for bankruptcy in 2012. The technology they invented destroyed them because they could not self-cannibalize.

**Blockbuster vs. Netflix:** Netflix offered its fledgling DVD-by-mail business to Blockbuster for $50 million in 2000. Blockbuster's CEO John Antioco reportedly laughed. Blockbuster had 9,000 stores, billions in revenue, and late fees were a profit center ($800 million annually at peak). The Netflix model eliminated late fees, required no physical stores, and initially had worse selection. Every metric that Blockbuster's management was incentivized to optimize said Netflix was irrelevant. Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy in 2010.

**Nokia:** In 2007, Nokia held 49.4% of global smartphone market share. By 2013, it had been acquired by Microsoft. Nokia's engineers knew about touchscreen smartphones -- internal documents show prototypes years before the iPhone. But Nokia's organizational structure was built around hardware manufacturing and carrier relationships. The software capability needed for a touchscreen OS was in a different department, with different leadership, different incentives, and different culture. Internal tribalism (see Section 2.5) compounded the inertia: the Symbian team, the MeeGo team, and the hardware teams were in active conflict over resources and strategic direction. A 2015 INSEAD case study by Vuori and Huy documented how fear and organizational politics, not ignorance of the market, drove Nokia's failure.

**Sears:** From its founding through the mid-20th century, Sears was the Amazon of its era -- a logistics and retail juggernaut that pioneered mail-order shopping, credit cards, and suburban retail. It had the infrastructure, the customer relationships, and the data to dominate e-commerce. Instead, it spent decades protecting its physical retail model. By the time it acknowledged the shift, it had neither the technology, the talent, nor the organizational agility to compete. Sears filed for bankruptcy in 2018.

**S&P 500 corporate lifespan data:**

The McKinsey/Innosight Corporate Longevity analysis has tracked the average tenure of companies on the S&P 500 index:

| Year | Average Tenure on S&P 500 (years) |
|------|-----------------------------------|
| 1964 | ~33 |
| 1980 | ~25 |
| 1998 | ~20 |
| 2012 | ~18 |
| 2020 | ~16 |
| 2027 (projected) | ~12 |

*Source: Innosight "Creative Destruction" reports (various years). Methodology: average tenure of companies on the S&P 500 index at the time of measurement.*

The trend is clear: the lifespan of large corporations is shrinking. Companies are being displaced faster. The hedges of scale and market position are eroding. But note: this data does not mean large companies are dying at a faster rate per se -- it means the competitive environment is churning faster, and organizations unable to adapt are being replaced more quickly.

- Vuori & Huy (2015), "Distributed Attention and Shared Emotions in the Innovation Process: How Nokia Lost the Smartphone Battle" (Administrative Science Quarterly)

- Foster & Kaplan (2001), "Creative Destruction: Why Companies That Are Built to Last Underperform the Market"

### 2.5 Siloing and Internal Tribalism (Tribalism)

**The human tendency:** In-group loyalty and out-group hostility -- among the oldest and deepest human tendencies, rooted in evolutionary psychology. Jonathan Haidt's research on moral psychology shows that loyalty to one's group is a foundational moral instinct, not a deviation from rationality. Henri Tajfel's minimal group experiments demonstrated that humans form in-group preferences based on the most trivial distinctions (random assignment to groups was sufficient).

**The hedge it defeats:** Organizational coordination, information flow, internal cooperation.

**The mechanism of failure:**

Corporate organizations are structured into departments, divisions, and teams. This structure is necessary -- it enables specialization and manageability. But each structural boundary becomes a tribal boundary. The engineering team, the sales team, the finance team, the legal team -- each develops its own culture, jargon, incentive structure, and worldview. Information that should flow freely across the organization gets trapped inside silos. Cooperation across boundaries becomes politically fraught.

The mechanism is self-reinforcing:

1. **Incentive misalignment.** Different departments are measured on different metrics. Sales is measured on revenue; engineering on quality and uptime; finance on cost control; legal on risk mitigation. Optimizing for one department's metrics often comes at another's expense.
2. **Budget competition.** Departments compete for finite budget. One team's gain is another team's loss. This creates a zero-sum internal economy that breeds adversarial relationships.
3. **Information as power.** Within a siloed organization, information is a source of power. Sharing information freely means giving up advantage. Hoarding information -- about customer problems, technical debt, market threats -- becomes individually rational even when collectively destructive.
4. **Identity formation.** People identify with their team. "We're engineering, they're business" becomes a core identity marker. This triggers all the dynamics of in-group/out-group psychology: favorable attribution to in-group members, hostile attribution to out-group members, distorted evaluation of cross-group proposals.

**Concrete examples:**

**Microsoft's "stack ranking" era (2000-2013):** Under CEO Steve Ballmer, Microsoft implemented a forced-ranking performance review system where every team had to rank its members and the bottom tier faced termination. The perverse incentive: employees were competing against their own teammates, not against external competitors. Kurt Eichenwald's 2012 Vanity Fair investigation ("Microsoft's Lost Decade") documented how this system destroyed collaboration. Engineers refused to work with talented people for fear of being ranked below them. Teams sabotaged each other. Information hoarding became a survival strategy. Microsoft missed the mobile revolution, cloud computing (initially), social media, and search during this period -- not for lack of talent, but because the internal environment prevented coordination. Eichenwald quoted multiple former employees who called stack ranking the single most destructive process in the company's history.

**Wells Fargo's cross-selling scandal (2016):** Wells Fargo's retail banking division created approximately 3.5 million fake accounts without customer consent. The root cause was a toxic combination of siloed incentives and top-down pressure. The retail banking division had aggressive cross-selling targets (the "Gr-eight" initiative to sell eight products to each customer). Branch employees who couldn't meet these targets faced termination. The compliance and risk management functions -- which existed precisely to prevent this kind of abuse -- were structurally subordinate to the revenue-generating divisions. Warnings from internal audit and compliance were documented but suppressed or minimized by retail banking leadership. The corporate structure created tribes (retail banking vs. risk management) with misaligned power and incentives, and the tribe with the revenue won until the fraud was too large to hide. The bank paid over $3 billion in fines and settlements.

**GM's ignition switch defect (2005-2014):** General Motors knew about a faulty ignition switch in its Cobalt and other models for at least a decade before issuing a recall. The defect was linked to at least 124 deaths. Internal investigations revealed that the problem was known to engineers but was never escalated effectively because it fell between organizational boundaries -- the design team, the manufacturing team, the legal team, and the recall process team each had pieces of the information but no one had the full picture, the authority to act, or the incentive to escalate across tribal boundaries. The Valukas Report (2014), commissioned by GM's board, described a "pattern of incompetence and neglect" driven by siloed decision-making and a culture where crossing organizational boundaries was not rewarded.

**Key research:**

- Tajfel (1970), minimal group paradigm experiments
- Haidt (2012), "The Righteous Mind" -- moral foundations of tribalism
- Eichenwald (2012), "Microsoft's Lost Decade" (Vanity Fair)
- Valukas (2014), GM Ignition Switch Investigation Report
- Bunderson & Reagans (2011), research on organizational subgroups and knowledge transfer

## 3. The Compounding -- How Cracks Interact and Amplify

The cracks described above do not operate in isolation. They form a system of reinforcing failures -- a corporate death spiral where each crack widens the others.

### 3.1 The Compounding Sequence

**Stage 1: C-suite captures governance.**

The board becomes captured (Section 2.1). Executive compensation is decoupled from long-term value creation. The CEO and senior leadership optimize for personal extraction -- stock-based compensation, golden parachutes, short-term earnings that inflate stock price.

**Stage 2: Extraction replaces investment.**

With governance captured, the firm shifts from "retain and reinvest" to "downsize and distribute" (Lazonick). R&D spending is cut or redirected to incremental improvements on existing products. Capital is redirected to share buybacks and dividends that enrich executives and shareholders at the expense of long-term capability. Between 2010 and 2019, S&P 500 companies spent over $5.3 trillion on share buybacks -- money that was not invested in innovation, worker training, or infrastructure.

**Stage 3: Moats replace value.**

As the firm's competitive edge dulls (because investment was redirected to extraction), it turns to rent-seeking (Section 2.2). Lobbying, patent accumulation, regulatory capture, and platform lock-in become the strategy. The firm no longer wins by being better -- it wins by making it harder for others to compete. This is rational from the C-suite's perspective: moats protect the revenue base that funds their compensation.

**Stage 4: Bureaucracy grows.**

The organization becomes internally focused (Section 2.3). Layers of management, compliance functions, and administrative overhead proliferate. Each new layer creates work for other layers. The Peter Principle fills management ranks with people who are competent at their previous role and incompetent at their current one. Empire-building incentives ensure the organization grows in headcount even as it stagnates in output.

**Stage 5: Silos calcify.**

Departments become tribes (Section 2.5). Internal competition replaces cooperation. Information stops flowing. The organization becomes a collection of fiefdoms defending their budgets and headcount rather than a coordinated enterprise serving customers.

**Stage 6: Inertia becomes fatal.**

The organization cannot adapt (Section 2.4). When a disruptive threat emerges, the organization has neither the agility to pivot (too bureaucratic), the investment capacity to innovate (money went to buybacks and moats), the information flow to recognize the threat early (siloed), nor the leadership willing to cannibalize existing revenue (C-suite is incentivized to protect the status quo that funds their compensation). The firm dies or is acquired.

**Stage 7: The cycle restarts.**

The disruptor that killed the incumbent begins the same cycle. It grows. It hires managers. It goes public. Agency problems emerge. The board becomes captured. The cycle repeats.

### 3.2 Evidence for the Cycle

**S&P 500 turnover:** As noted in Section 2.4, the average tenure on the S&P 500 has declined from approximately 33 years in 1964 to approximately 16 years in 2020, with projections suggesting further decline. This is the aggregate signature of the corporate lifecycle accelerating.

**Corporate lifespan research:** A 2016 study by the Santa Fe Institute (Daepp, Hamilton, West, and Bettencourt) analyzed the lifespans of 25,000+ publicly traded North American companies from 1950 to 2009. They found that, regardless of industry, the hazard rate (probability of death in any given year) was approximately constant at around 10% per year for companies that survived beyond a few years. This means the "half-life" of a publicly traded company is approximately 10 years. The corporate form does not become more durable with age -- if anything, the oldest companies die at the same rate as younger ones. The researchers concluded that corporate mortality follows a pattern similar to biological organisms: there is no "immunity" gained from longevity.

**GE as archetype:** General Electric's trajectory from the 1980s through the 2020s is the compounding sequence in miniature. Under Welch, governance was captured by a charismatic CEO who was celebrated rather than checked. Extraction increased (Welch's personal compensation, GE Capital's financial engineering). Moat-building occurred through conglomerate diversification and lobbying. Bureaucracy grew enormous. Silos between GE's many divisions prevented coordination. When the financial crisis hit, GE could not adapt. The company that was once the most valuable in the world was dismembered and sold for parts.

### 3.3 Is the Corporate Form Inherently Cyclical?

The evidence suggests yes -- with important qualifications.

**The lifecycle pattern appears robust:** Born lean and hungry, the firm solves real coordination problems and creates genuine value. As it succeeds and grows, the human-nature tendencies it was designed to hedge against find their way through the cracks. The organization becomes increasingly oriented toward self-preservation (of the people and structures within it) rather than value creation (for customers and society). Eventually, the accumulated friction makes it unable to adapt, and it is displaced.

**But the speed varies enormously.** Some companies (Toyota, Berkshire Hathaway, certain family-controlled firms) have maintained vitality for decades beyond the statistical norm. This suggests the death spiral is not inevitable on any fixed timeline -- it can be accelerated by poor governance or decelerated by structural features that resist the cracks (see Section 4).

**And the replacement cycle works.** The corporate form's great strength is that the competitive system as a whole is self-correcting even though individual firms are not. Kodak dies, but photography continues (and improves). Blockbuster dies, but entertainment distribution continues (and improves). The waste is in the transition -- the jobs lost, the capital destroyed, the innovation delayed. The question for the Agent Commons project is whether a different organizational form can reduce this waste while preserving the creative destruction that drives progress.

## 4. Corporate Structures That Resist (or Try To)

Not all corporate structures are equally susceptible. Several alternative forms attempt to patch specific cracks.

### 4.1 B-Corps and Benefit Corporations

**What crack they address:** C-suite extraction and short-termism. By legally encoding a purpose beyond shareholder profit maximization, B-Corps attempt to create a governance structure that prevents the optimization-for-extraction spiral.

**How they work:** B-Corp certification (from B Lab, a nonprofit) requires meeting social and environmental performance standards, legal accountability to stakeholders beyond shareholders, and public transparency. Benefit corporations (a legal structure, distinct from B-Corp certification) allow directors to consider impacts on workers, community, and environment -- not just shareholders -- when making decisions.

**Effectiveness evidence:** As of 2024, there are over 7,000 certified B-Corps globally. Research on their performance is limited but suggestive. A 2019 study by Cao, Liang, and Zhan found that B-Corps showed higher employee satisfaction, lower turnover, and comparable financial performance to matched peers. However, the self-selection problem is severe: companies that pursue B-Corp certification are already run by people who care about these issues. The question is whether the structure prevents drift over time, especially after founder departure. Patagonia's conversion to a "purpose trust" structure (see 4.2) suggests that even B-Corp status was not considered sufficient long-term protection against extraction.

**New problems:** B-Corp certification is voluntary and can be abandoned. The standards are set by B Lab, a private nonprofit, not by law. There is no enforcement mechanism beyond decertification. This makes B-Corp a weak hedge -- more of a signaling device than a structural protection.

### 4.2 Steward Ownership

**What crack they address:** The root cause of the C-suite extraction cycle -- the tradability and concentrability of ownership. If ownership can be accumulated, it will be. If it can be sold, the highest bidder is often the one with the most extractive intent.

**How they work:** Steward-owned companies are structured so that voting control is held by a trust or foundation that cannot sell the company, cannot extract profits beyond a reasonable return, and must maintain the company's mission. Profits are either reinvested or distributed to stakeholders (workers, community), not accumulated by owners.

**Examples:**

- **Robert Bosch GmbH:** 92% owned by the Robert Bosch Foundation. Revenue over EUR 90 billion (2023). One of the world's largest private companies. The foundation cannot sell the company and must use dividends for charitable purposes. This structure has been in place since 1964. Bosch has maintained R&D spending at approximately 8-9% of revenue -- significantly above the industrial average -- because there is no shareholder pressure to distribute profits or inflate stock prices.

- **Carl Zeiss Foundation:** Owns both Carl Zeiss AG and SCHOTT AG. Established in 1889 by Ernst Abbe, a physicist who believed that workers should share in the value they create. The foundation's charter prohibits sale of the companies and requires that profits be used for scientific research, education, and social purposes. Both companies are global leaders in their fields (optics and specialty glass, respectively) over 130 years later.

- **Patagonia:** In September 2022, founder Yvon Chouinard transferred all ownership of Patagonia (valued at approximately $3 billion) to a purpose trust (the Patagonia Purpose Trust, which holds all voting stock) and a nonprofit (the Holdfast Collective, which holds all nonvoting stock and receives all profits not reinvested in the business). The explicit intent was to make the company "un-sellable" and "un-extractable." Chouinard stated: "Instead of extracting value from nature and transforming it into wealth for investors, we'll use the wealth Patagonia creates to protect the source of all wealth."

**Effectiveness evidence:** The track record of steward-owned companies is remarkably strong. A 2020 study by the Purpose Foundation (which advocates for steward ownership) analyzed 50+ steward-owned companies across Europe and found that they outperformed comparable conventional firms in longevity, R&D investment, employee retention, and customer satisfaction. The German Mittelstand -- the small and medium-sized enterprises that form the backbone of German manufacturing -- includes many family-controlled or foundation-owned firms with steward-like characteristics, and they have dramatically outperformed comparable public companies in long-term innovation and market stability.

**Limitations:** Steward ownership requires a founder willing to give up personal wealth (the Chouinard example is exceptional -- most founders won't transfer $3 billion to a trust). It is difficult to retrofit into an existing publicly traded company. And it creates its own governance challenge: who controls the trust/foundation? If the trust's board can be captured, the cycle simply moves up one level.

### 4.3 Partnership Models

**What crack they address:** Agency problems (principal-agent misalignment). In a partnership, the owners are also the workers. There is no separation of ownership and control -- the people making decisions are the people bearing the consequences.

**How they work:** Law firms, consulting firms, accounting firms, and private equity firms are typically structured as partnerships where senior professionals ("partners") share ownership, profits, and governance responsibility.

**Effectiveness evidence:** Partnerships do reduce certain agency problems. When your own money is on the line, you pay more attention to risk management. When your reputation is directly tied to the firm's output, you maintain quality. The major consulting firms (McKinsey, BCG, Bain), law firms (Cravath, Wachtell), and accounting firms (the Big Four) have maintained quality and longevity across decades under partnership structures.

**New problems:** Partnerships create their own class system -- partners vs. associates. The path to partner is a tournament that incentivizes overwork, conformity, and the same status-seeking that corporate hierarchy produces. Partners may become a rentier class, extracting disproportionate value from the labor of junior associates. Firms like McKinsey and Goldman Sachs (before its 1999 IPO) have been criticized for creating internal feudal systems that are at least as hierarchical as traditional corporations, just with different labels.

Additionally, partnerships struggle to raise capital. Without equity shares to sell, growth capital must come from partner contributions or retained earnings. This limits scale. The trend among large professional firms has been away from pure partnership toward corporate structures (Goldman Sachs going public, Big Four firms adopting corporate-like structures) -- evidence that the partnership model's limitations drive convergence back toward the corporate form.

### 4.4 Founder-Led Companies

**What crack they address:** Short-termism, bureaucratic bloat, and governance capture. Founders typically have a long-term vision, deep product knowledge, and the authority to override bureaucratic inertia.

**How they work:** The founder retains significant control (often through dual-class share structures that give them disproportionate voting power) and continues to lead the company through its growth phase and beyond.

**Effectiveness evidence:** Research on founder-led performance is substantial and generally positive. A 2016 study by Fahlenbrach found that founder-CEO firms had higher Tobin's Q (a measure of market valuation relative to assets), higher ROA, and higher stock returns than comparable non-founder firms. Bain & Company research found that founder-led S&P 500 companies generated 3.1x the returns of the overall index between 1990 and 2014.

The logic is straightforward: founders have intrinsic motivation beyond financial compensation (identity, legacy, genuine passion for the product). They can take long-term bets that a professional CEO, measured on quarterly earnings, would never take. They cut through bureaucracy because they have the authority and the mandate. Amazon under Bezos, Apple under Jobs (in his second tenure), Meta under Zuckerberg, and NVIDIA under Huang are prominent examples.

**New problems:** Founder-led companies carry significant "key person risk." Apple nearly died when Jobs was ousted in 1985 and was resurrected only when he returned in 1997. When founders burn out, lose touch, or simply age out, the company faces a succession crisis for which the founder-led structure provides no solution. Furthermore, unchecked founder authority is the flip side of the governance capture problem: if the board cannot check the CEO because the CEO controls the board through dual-class shares, the founder's judgment becomes the single point of failure. Travis Kalanick at Uber, Adam Neumann at WeWork, and Elizabeth Holmes at Theranos illustrate how founder authority without accountability can lead to disaster.

**The paradox:** Founder-led companies resist bureaucratic decay longer but are more vulnerable to individual human failings. They trade systemic failure (the death spiral) for idiosyncratic failure (bad founder decisions).

### 4.5 Open-Book Management and Radical Transparency

**What crack they address:** Information hoarding, siloing, and the opacity that enables extraction and misalignment.

**How they work:** Companies like Semco (Ricardo Semler), Buffer, and Bridgewater Associates practice various forms of radical transparency -- open salaries, open financials, open decision-making processes. The theory is that when everyone can see everything, the information asymmetry that enables exploitation disappears.

**Effectiveness evidence:** Semco's experiment in democratic management (workers set their own salaries, vote on major decisions, have access to all financial information) generated significant revenue growth and profitability from the 1980s through the 2000s. Buffer's open salary model has been associated with high employee satisfaction and low turnover. Bridgewater's "radical transparency" (all meetings recorded, all critiques public) is credited by Ray Dalio with enabling better decision-making.

**New problems:** Radical transparency can create a surveillance culture. Bridgewater's model has been criticized by former employees as psychologically brutal. Open salaries can create resentment and gaming rather than fairness. And transparency does not prevent power dynamics -- it just makes them visible. Knowing that the CEO makes 50x your salary is not the same as having the power to change it.

### 4.6 Remote and Distributed Companies

**What crack they address:** Physical hierarchy and the informal power dynamics of in-person work (proximity to power, hallway decisions, presence-based evaluation).

**How they work:** Companies like GitLab, Zapier, Automattic, and Basecamp operate with fully distributed teams, relying on asynchronous communication, written documentation, and output-based evaluation rather than face-time-based management.

**Effectiveness evidence:** GitLab has scaled to over 1,500 team members while maintaining a fully remote structure. Its publicly accessible handbook (over 2,000 pages) documents everything, reducing the information asymmetry that enables siloing and politics. Remote-first companies report hiring advantages (access to global talent pool), lower overhead (no office costs), and in some studies, higher productivity.

**New problems:** Remote work creates its own pathologies. Isolation, difficulty building trust, "Zoom fatigue," and the challenge of informal mentoring and culture transmission. Async communication can be slow for coordination-intensive tasks. And informal hierarchies still emerge -- they just operate through Slack channels, meeting invitations, and response times rather than corner offices.

### 4.7 Summary: What Patches What

| Structure | Crack Addressed | Effectiveness | New Problems |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|--------------|
| B-Corp | Extraction, short-termism | Moderate (voluntary, weak enforcement) | Certification can be abandoned |
| Steward ownership | Extraction, ownership concentration | Strong (structural, legally enforced) | Hard to retrofit; trust governance |
| Partnership | Principal-agent misalignment | Moderate (owners = operators) | Creates partner/associate class system |
| Founder-led | Short-termism, bureaucratic bloat | Strong while founder is capable | Key-person risk, succession crisis |
| Open-book management | Information asymmetry, siloing | Moderate (visibility does not equal power) | Surveillance culture, gaming |
| Remote/distributed | Physical hierarchy, proximity politics | Moderate (reduces some political dynamics) | Isolation, trust-building, culture |

**The key insight:** Every alternative structure addresses one or two cracks while potentially opening others. No single structural innovation resolves the full compounding sequence. This suggests the problem is not any specific organizational design but the underlying human tendencies that find their way through any design.

## 5. The Coase Question in the AI Age

### 5.1 Revisiting the Logic of the Firm

If firms exist because of transaction costs (Coase), and AI dramatically reduces transaction costs, then the equilibrium size of firms should shrink. This is the most important structural prediction for the Agent Commons thesis.

**What AI reduces:**

1. **Search costs.** Finding the right person/supplier for a task. AI can match needs to capabilities across global markets in seconds. The cost of searching the "market" for talent drops precipitously.

2. **Information processing costs.** Understanding complex information, making decisions, analyzing data. A single person with AI can process information that previously required an analytical team.

3. **Coordination costs.** Keeping multiple people aligned. AI tools (shared context, automated summaries, real-time translation, project management) reduce the overhead of coordinating across organizational boundaries.

4. **Monitoring costs.** Verifying that work is done correctly. AI can review code, check documents, validate data, and assess quality at a fraction of the cost of human review.

5. **Communication costs.** AI reduces language barriers, summarizes long discussions, extracts action items, and translates between technical and business contexts.

**What this implies for firm size:**

If the Coasian logic holds, and AI reduces transaction costs by an order of magnitude, the optimal firm size should shrink dramatically. Many functions currently performed inside firms -- legal analysis, financial modeling, marketing, software development, customer support -- become viable as external services coordinated through market mechanisms rather than internal hierarchy.

### 5.2 Evidence Already Emerging

**The "1000x engineer" phenomenon:** AI coding assistants (GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code) are enabling individual developers or very small teams to build products that previously required teams of 10-100. Anecdotal but widespread reports from the developer community suggest 2-10x productivity improvements for experienced developers using AI tools, with some tasks (boilerplate code, documentation, testing) seeing even larger gains. If a team of 3 can do what a team of 30 did, the Coasian logic for keeping those 30 people inside a firm evaporates.

**AI-native startups:** A new generation of startups is being built with dramatically smaller teams than previous generations:

- Midjourney reportedly generated over $200 million in annual revenue with fewer than 40 full-time employees (as of mid-2023).
- Instagram had 13 employees when it was acquired for $1 billion in 2012 -- an early harbinger of the pattern, pre-generative AI.
- WhatsApp had 55 employees when it was acquired for $19 billion in 2014.
- More recently, AI-native companies are launching products with single-digit teams that compete with established players employing hundreds.

**Corporate restructuring:** Large technology companies began significant layoffs in 2022-2024, with explicit connections to AI. Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, and others collectively laid off hundreds of thousands of workers. While macroeconomic factors played a role, executives repeatedly cited AI-driven efficiency as a structural rationale. Meta's "Year of Efficiency" (2023) explicitly linked headcount reductions to AI augmentation of remaining workers.

**The solopreneur explosion:** Platforms like Substack, Shopify, Gumroad, and various AI tools have enabled individuals to build media businesses, e-commerce operations, and software products as solo operators or with minimal teams. This is the logical endpoint of the Coasian prediction: when transaction costs approach zero and AI provides capabilities that used to require an organization, the firm size approaches one.

### 5.3 What Can't Be Disaggregated (Yet)

Honest analysis requires acknowledging where the corporate form retains structural advantages that AI does not (yet) erode:

1. **Physical manufacturing.** Making physical things at scale still requires capital, supply chains, factories, and coordination that benefits from firm organization. AI improves efficiency but does not eliminate the need for physical infrastructure and the people who operate it.

2. **Regulated industries.** Healthcare, finance, aviation, pharmaceuticals -- industries where regulatory compliance requires organizational infrastructure that individual operators cannot provide. (Though AI may eventually reduce these compliance costs too.)

3. **Deep capital requirements.** Semiconductor fabrication, space launch, drug development -- ventures requiring billions in upfront investment still need organizational vehicles for capital aggregation. Limited liability remains essential here.

4. **Network effects and platform economics.** Some products become more valuable with more users (social networks, marketplaces, operating systems). This creates natural monopolies that resist disaggregation -- not because of coordination costs but because of demand-side economies of scale.

5. **Trust and reputation.** Large organizations provide a trust shorthand. "IBM" or "McKinsey" carries reputational weight that a solo operator or small team cannot easily match. AI may help with quality verification, but brand trust is a social phenomenon that technology alone does not replace.

### 5.4 New Coordination Needs

The Coasian prediction -- that AI should shrink firms -- may be partially offset by new coordination challenges that AI creates:

- **AI governance and safety.** As AI systems become more powerful, coordinating their development and deployment may require organizational structures that don't yet exist.
- **Data coordination.** AI models require large datasets. Coordinating data collection, labeling, privacy compliance, and model training may create new reasons for firm-like organization.
- **AI-human interface design.** Making AI useful requires understanding human needs, designing interactions, and building trust -- work that may not reduce with AI augmentation.

### 5.5 The Implication for Agent Commons

If the Coasian logic is correct, the AI age creates a fundamental opening for new organizational forms. The firm was the answer to a specific question: how do you coordinate human economic activity when market transactions are too expensive? If AI makes market-like coordination cheap enough, the question changes. The new question becomes: what is the minimal organizational structure needed to coordinate AI-augmented humans working on shared goals?

This is precisely the question the Agent Commons concept asks. The three-layer model (Agent, Forg, Commons) can be understood as a minimal organizational structure designed for a world where:

- Transaction costs are low (AI handles search, coordination, monitoring).
- Firm-specific capital is less important (AI democratizes capability).
- The human-nature cracks documented above still exist and need hedging.
- But the hedges can be different because AI can play the role that organizational hierarchy once played.

## 7. Honest Assessment -- What Corporations Do Well

Any analysis of corporate failure that does not acknowledge corporate success is intellectually dishonest. The corporate form:

1. **Enabled unprecedented coordination.** The Apollo program, the internet, global supply chains, modern medicine -- all were built by or through corporate organizations (sometimes in partnership with government). No other organizational form has demonstrated the ability to coordinate millions of people across continents toward complex goals.

2. **Created the middle class.** Stable employment with benefits, pensions, and career progression was the economic engine of mid-20th-century prosperity. The corporate employment contract, for all its problems, provided economic security that market-only coordination never has.

3. **Drove relentless innovation through competition.** The creative destruction cycle, for all its waste, produced exponential improvements in technology, medicine, agriculture, and quality of life. The profit motive, channeled through competition, is the most powerful innovation engine yet discovered.

4. **Provided a vehicle for capital aggregation.** Limited liability corporations enabled the pooling of small investments into large ventures that no individual could fund. This capital formation mechanism built the modern world's infrastructure.

5. **Scales.** Unlike cooperatives, commons, or partnerships, the corporate form has demonstrated the ability to operate at enormous scale -- hundreds of thousands of employees, global operations, multi-decade lifespans. This scaling ability, even if it degrades over time, is not replicated by any alternative form.

**The failure pattern is real, but so is the success pattern.** The honest conclusion is not that corporations are broken -- it is that they are a powerful but decaying solution. They solve coordination problems brilliantly at first, then gradually succumb to the human-nature tendencies they were designed to hedge against. The question for the Agent Commons project is whether a new form can retain the coordination power while resisting the decay.

## 9. Open Questions for Further Research

1. **Is the compounding sequence deterministic or probabilistic?** Can specific interventions at specific stages reliably prevent the death spiral, or is decay inevitable once a firm reaches a certain size/age?

2. **What is the role of culture?** German Mittelstand firms and Japanese keiretsu have shown different decay patterns than Anglo-American corporations. Is this cultural, structural, or both?

3. **Do the Coasian dynamics actually unravel?** Or do new coordination costs (AI governance, data coordination, regulatory compliance) create new reasons for firm-like organization even as old ones disappear?

4. **Can AI serve as a hedge?** If human boards fail because they are human (subject to groupthink, capture, information asymmetry), could AI governance systems serve as a more reliable check? What are the risks?

5. **Is there a minimum viable hedge set?** Across all the cracks documented above, is there a core set of structural features that, if implemented, would resist the compounding sequence? Or must each crack be patched individually?

6. **What happens to labor?** If AI reduces optimal firm size, what happens to the billions of people who currently depend on corporate employment for economic security? The Agent Commons concept must address this -- not just as a coordination problem but as a human welfare problem.
